Finding Coffee Roasteries in Denmark

The year is 2024. I received a gift card to an espresso-making course for my birthday. As I have considered myself something of a coffee lover for a good 35 years now, I did not think I had all that much to learn, but I went ahead with an open mind.

Not only did I find out I knew much less about coffee than imagined, there were flavour profiles to espresso that I never knew existed. Sticking to the big brands purchased in bulk by discount had, up until then, been where my experimentation of espresso ended.

Another thing: I used to prefer French Roast. I have since learned that French Roast is fancy speak for "burnt to a crisp". And after 6 months of drinking nothing but high quality freshly roasted beans, I wonder how on earth I found French Roast to be even remotely adequate.

In the course we spent a good hour doing nothing but testing how the grinding setting changed the flavour profile of the amazing freshly roasted beans from Coffee Collective in Aarhus. The reason I personally do not make too much of a fuss about the perfect grinding setting is simply: I am too cheap. I refuse to throw away a brewed espresso at home even if it tastes like battery acid, especially since starting to purchase nothing but quality freshly roasted beans. With shipment the cost is usually about 130 DKK a pop.

But I digress.

Initially, after the coffee workshop, I got a monthly subscription of espresso from Coffee Collective, which was great. But then I started looking for other coffee roasters in Denmark and discovered that there are well over 50 independent roasteries — in this little collection of islands that hosts some 6 million people.

Obviously I had to set off on a path and try them all. So I dug up all the online roasteries I could find and set off to purchase at least one bag of coffee from each one.

My requirement list is as follows:

  • It has to be espresso
  • Payment has to be possible via Apple Pay or MobilePay (my money, my rules)
  • I want the beans whole, to grind myself

My scoring system is as follows:

  • I will try out various grinding settings on my Sage Barista Pro until I find the one I like the most. This is done for each bag and I mark the bag with my favorite setting
  • I prefer lattes or cappuccino over espresso. Every coffee bag was tested with skimmed milk (0.2%), whole milk (3.5%), and Oatly Barista iKaffe
  • I live in Fyn and we have extremely hard water here. I use a softener filter and religiously clean my machine. But it does affect coffee flavour. Some of the coffee I liked less may have flourished with softer water. Some of the coffees may have been the exact opposite. Not much I can do about that

There are a total of N=1 participants in these coffee tests. And said participant is too cheap to throw away even a bad cup.

I have purchased coffee from about half of the places on my list now after I started this project. I must admit that I have cheated a little and repeatedly bought a few favourites instead of buying from a new place every month.

Every single roastery so far has had outstanding service. I get everything from a little written smile on the receipt to a small caramel or coffee sticker. Frankly, I have never received such good service purchasing a kilo of Lavazza in a regular store.

I rate my favourites with espresso cups — from one cup (solid) to three cups (outstanding). It may very well be that I simply never found the correct grind setting for the ones I didn't like as much, and ruined the coffee. We will never know.

I stand by my choices and will update as I continue to find more roasteries.
